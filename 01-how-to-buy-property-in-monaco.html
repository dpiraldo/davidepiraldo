How to buy property in Monaco: the complete process

Davide Piraldo·July 2026·7 min read
editorial photograph — Carré d'Or, Monaco

Monaco places no restrictions on foreign ownership — anyone can buy, regardless of nationality or residency status. What surprises most international buyers isn't eligibility, it's speed: an accepted offer becomes a binding deposit within days, not weeks.

The transaction is anchored by a Monegasque notary, a Prince-appointed official whose role is closer to a neutral judicial authority than a conveyancer. Every sale of Monaco real estate passes through a notary's office, and the fees are fixed by scale rather than negotiated.

The process, step by step

  • Written offer. Once a property is identified, the buyer's agent submits a formal written offer with price and a response deadline.
  • Preliminary agreement. If accepted, the notary drafts a promesse or compromis de vente, setting out any conditions — financing, title checks, planning matters.
  • Cooling-off & deposit. A short cooling-off period follows, after which the buyer wires a 10% deposit into the notary's escrow account, which legally binds the sale.
  • Government pre-emption window. The Monegasque state holds a pre-emptive right to purchase certain properties; this must lapse or be waived, typically within about a month, before the sale can complete.
  • Title & diligence. The notary verifies title, planning history, and beneficial-ownership documentation — a step that has grown stricter since Monaco's 2023 anti-money-laundering reforms.
  • Signing & completion. Both parties sign the acte de vente, the balance and fees are paid, and the notary registers the new owner with the land registry.
"The 10% deposit is the moment the deal becomes real — everything before it is negotiation, everything after it is paperwork."

What it costs beyond the price

For an individual buyer or a qualifying Monaco civil company (SCI), total transaction costs — registration duty plus notary fees — run to approximately 6% of the purchase price. Foreign corporate structures pay more: registration duty rises to 7.5% for transparent entities and up to 10% where beneficial ownership isn't fully disclosed, a direct consequence of Monaco's tightened compliance framework.

New-build purchases follow a different regime: 20% VAT applies in place of registration duty, with a reduced notary fee — relevant for buyers considering Mareterra or other recent developments.

Agency commission sits outside these figures: customarily 5% (plus VAT) paid by the seller and 3% (plus VAT) paid by the buyer, though arrangements vary deal to deal.
