Why Monaco real estate holds its value

Davide Piraldo·July 2026·5 min read
editorial photograph — aerial view, Monaco coastline

Monaco covers just over two square kilometres. Every buyer, every developer, and every resident competes for a fixed and essentially unexpandable supply of land — the single fact that underwrites the entire market.

Resale volume across the Principality reached €3.2 billion in 2025, a 49.1% increase on the prior year and the clearest signal yet that Monaco's secondary market has entered a new cycle. Transaction counts rose too — 429 resales recorded across the eight districts, up 17.5% year over year.

Liquidity is concentrated, not evenly spread

Monte-Carlo alone accounted for 164 resales worth €1.1 billion in 2025 — the first time the district has crossed the billion-euro mark, with resale value up more than 40% year over year. At the other extreme, Larvotto recorded only 13 resales — yet still moved €851.9 million, an average of roughly €65.5 million per sale. That's a thin market by transaction count and an extraordinarily deep one by value: pricing power in Larvotto sits firmly with sellers.

"Thirteen resales moving €851.9 million isn't a slow market — it's a market where scarcity has already done the pricing work."

The land-reclamation exception

Mareterra, the six-hectare extension completed at Anse du Portier, is the rare instance of genuinely new supply entering the market. Even there, land scarcity reasserts itself: reported entry prices for the smaller apartments start near €100,000 per square metre, among the highest values for any residential development globally.

For buyers weighing Monaco against other Mediterranean markets, the practical takeaway is straightforward: value preservation in Monaco isn't driven by a growth story in the conventional sense — it's driven by a supply constraint that no amount of demand can resolve.
