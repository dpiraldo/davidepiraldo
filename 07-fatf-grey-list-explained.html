The FATF grey list, explained simply

Davide Piraldo·July 2026·5 min read
editorial photograph — Monaco banking quarter

Prospective buyers occasionally come across headlines about Monaco and the "grey list" and assume the worst. The reality is narrower, more technical, and far less alarming than it sounds — but it deserves a clear explanation rather than a dismissal.

What the grey list actually is

The Financial Action Task Force (FATF) is the Paris-based intergovernmental body that sets global standards on anti-money-laundering and counter-terrorist-financing controls. Its "grey list" — formally, "Jurisdictions Under Increased Monitoring" — names countries that have committed to fixing identified gaps in those controls, on an agreed timetable. It sits well below the FATF's actual blacklist (currently Iran, North Korea and Myanmar), which triggers far more serious countermeasures.

Why Monaco is on it

Monaco was placed under increased monitoring in June 2024, following a MONEYVAL evaluation that found the effectiveness of its anti-money-laundering system "uneven" — not because laws were missing, but because enforcement outcomes (prosecutions, asset seizures, sanctions actually applied) hadn't yet been demonstrated at scale.

The distinction that matters
Grey-listing is a statement about financial-crime enforcement capacity. It is not a statement about tax status, legal certainty, or the safety of owning property in Monaco.

What does not change

Monaco's tax framework is untouched — the zero personal income tax regime, registration duties, and inheritance rules described elsewhere on this Journal remain exactly as they were. Foreign ownership of real estate remains unrestricted. Monaco's legal and judicial framework for property transactions has not been altered by the listing.

"The grey list changes how carefully a bank checks your paperwork — not what you owe, or what you're allowed to buy."

What actually does change

The practical impact sits almost entirely in banking compliance: enhanced due diligence on wire transfers, account openings, and the documentation required around real estate transactions and beneficial ownership. Buyers should expect somewhat more paperwork and longer processing times at the banking stage of a purchase — not a different set of rules for the purchase itself.
