Monaco taxes: what you actually pay

Davide Piraldo·July 2026·6 min read
editorial photograph — Monaco harbour skyline

Zero personal income tax has been the rule in Monaco since 1869, and it remains true today. But "no income tax" is not the same as "no taxes" — several levies still apply, and one nationality is treated differently from every other.

What is genuinely zero

For non-French residents, personal income tax, capital gains tax, wealth tax and annual property tax are all 0%. There is no equivalent of a council tax or an annual levy simply for owning a home in the Principality.

The French exception

French nationals resident in Monaco are the one group excluded from this picture. Under the 1963 Franco-Monegasque fiscal convention, they remain liable for French income tax as if they were resident in France — a detail that surprises many French buyers who assume Monaco residency itself is the tax event.

Two conditions, not one

To benefit from the zero-income-tax regime, an individual needs both administrative residency (the Carte de Séjour) and a separate Monaco tax residency certificate, which generally requires spending more than 183 days per year in the Principality or having one's centre of economic interests there. Buying a property does not, by itself, satisfy either requirement.
