A guide to Monaco's districts

Davide Piraldo·July 2026·7 min read
editorial photograph — Monaco, view across the quarters

Monaco is eight districts packed into two square kilometres, and each has a distinct character, price point and buyer profile. Choosing where to live matters as much as choosing what to buy.

Monte-Carlo

Monte-Carlo

The Principality's most recognisable quarter — Casino, Hôtel de Paris, the Grand Prix circuit itself. Consistently the highest transaction volume of any district, with 164 resales worth €1.1 billion in 2025 — the first time the district has crossed the billion-euro mark. Best suited to buyers who want to be at the centre of Monaco's social and commercial life.

Carré d'Or

Carré d'Or

The "Golden Square," Monaco's most prestigious shopping and dining quarter, sitting inside Monte-Carlo. Prices per square metre here are consistently among the highest recorded in the Principality — a favourite for buyers prioritising address and proximity over square footage.

Fontvieille

Fontvieille

Built largely on reclaimed land in the 1970s–80s, Fontvieille is greener and quieter, with the Princess Grace Rose Garden and the marina as its anchors. A strong fit for families, offering more modern building stock at a relative discount to Monte-Carlo.

Larvotto

Larvotto

Monaco's beach district, and now home to Mareterra, the Principality's newest waterfront extension. A genuinely thin market — only 13 resales in 2025 — but one of the deepest by value, averaging roughly €65.5 million per sale.

La Condamine

La Condamine

The harbour quarter, home to the historic port and Monaco's daily market. More residential in feel, with a broader range of transaction sizes and a growing base of long-term residents rather than pure investors.

"Larvotto and Monte-Carlo tell two different stories about the same city: one is scarcity by value, the other is scarcity by volume."

Two smaller quarters round out the picture: Moneghetti, a hillside residential area prized for its panoramic views, and La Rousse/Saint Roman, offering more accessible entry points alongside modern apartment stock — increasingly popular with buyers who want Monaco without competing for Monte-Carlo's ceiling prices.
