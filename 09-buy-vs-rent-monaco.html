Buying vs renting in Monaco

Davide Piraldo·July 2026·5 min read
editorial photograph — residential terrace, Monaco

Both paths lead to the same residency requirement — a genuine home in Monaco — but the financial and lifestyle trade-offs differ enough to deserve an honest comparison, not a sales pitch for either side.

Renting

  • Fastest route to satisfying the residency accommodation requirement
  • Minimum 12-month lease typical; tenant pays a 1% leasehold duty on total rent
  • No exposure to Monaco's registration duties or notary fees
  • Flexibility to test a district before committing capital
  • Rents in prime districts move quickly — decisions are often made in days

Buying

  • No annual property tax or capital gains tax for individuals
  • Total acquisition costs of roughly 6% (individual/SCI) at closing
  • Strengthens a residency application, though it isn't strictly required
  • Exposure to a market with structural scarcity and historically strong value retention
  • Illiquid relative to renting — capital is committed for the medium to long term
"Renting answers the question 'can I live here.' Buying answers a different question: 'do I want Monaco in my portfolio.'"

When renting first makes sense

For buyers new to Monaco, renting for a year before purchasing is a common and sensible sequence. It satisfies the residency accommodation requirement immediately, avoids committing capital before you've lived in a district day-to-day, and leaves the acquisition decision for when you actually know which quarter — Monte-Carlo's energy, Fontvieille's calm, Larvotto's beachfront — fits your life.

When buying makes more sense from day one

For buyers who already know Monaco well, or who are acquiring primarily as a long-term store of value rather than a primary residence, purchasing directly avoids paying rent twice — once now, once implicitly through the opportunity cost of capital sitting on the sidelines in a market where supply cannot expand to meet demand.
